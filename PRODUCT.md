# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Developers evaluating or already using TinaCMS: frontend engineers, full-stack developers, and content teams who want a visual editing CMS that works with Git-backed content. They range from solo devs building Jamstack sites to teams at agencies and companies managing documentation and marketing sites. Their context is usually a code editor in one tab, the Tina.io site in another, comparing options or looking up API details.

## Product Purpose

Tina.io is the public face of TinaCMS: the place where developers discover the project, understand what it does, read documentation, follow the blog, and decide whether to adopt it. Success looks like a developer landing on the site, quickly understanding what TinaCMS offers, finding the docs they need, and leaving confident enough to try it. The site should feel like the product it represents: open-source, developer-friendly, and genuinely useful.

## Brand Personality

**Friendly, bold, approachable.** The voice is direct and warm without being cutesy. Technical accuracy matters, but jargon walls don't. The llama mascot sets the tone: a project that takes its craft seriously but doesn't take itself too seriously. Energy over formality. Clarity over cleverness.

## Anti-references

- **Generic SaaS landing pages.** Cookie-cutter hero + features + pricing grids with stock gradients and abstract blob illustrations. If it could be any product by swapping the logo, it's wrong.
- **Enterprise/corporate.** Overly formal, muted, committee-designed. Salesforce/Adobe enterprise energy. Dense feature matrices and "contact sales" energy.
- **Hacker/terminal aesthetic.** Dark mode with neon accents, monospace everything, "built for 10x devs" gatekeeping energy. TinaCMS is for everyone who builds on the web.
- **Overdesigned startup.** Excessive animation, gratuitous 3D elements, parallax overload, style over substance. The design should serve the content, not compete with it.

## Design Principles

1. **Show the product, not a pitch.** Lead with real interfaces, real code, real editing experiences. Developers trust what they can see working, not marketing claims.
2. **Warm, not loud.** The orange palette and playful mascot create energy, but the site should feel inviting, not overwhelming. Confidence expressed through clarity, not volume.
3. **Docs are first-class.** Documentation is as important as the landing page. It deserves the same design attention, not a stripped-down template. A developer spending 30 minutes in the docs should feel as considered as someone spending 30 seconds on the homepage.
4. **Respect the developer's time.** Fast pages, scannable content, clear navigation. No friction between a developer and the information they need. Every interaction should feel efficient.
5. **Open-source spirit.** Transparent, community-oriented, welcoming to contributors. The site should feel like it belongs to its community, not to a sales team.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG AA compliance. 4.5:1 contrast ratios for text, keyboard navigable, screen reader friendly. Respect reduced-motion preferences. The site serves a global developer audience across two locales (English and Chinese); design should accommodate both without treating either as secondary.
